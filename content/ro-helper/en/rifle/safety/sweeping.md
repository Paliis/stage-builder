## What this is

**Sweeping** means that during the **course of fire (COF)** the **muzzle** crosses the competitor’s own body (arms, legs, torso, etc.)—the firearm is “swept” across body parts. This is unsafe handling and is normally treated as a **specific** breach under **10.5.5** rather than a vague **10.5.1**, when the facts match the rule text.

## What the Range Officer (RO) does

1. **Stop the hazard:** loud **“STOP!”**, control **muzzle direction**, maintain safe distance.
2. **Classify:** if facts also fit **180°**, **finger in register**, **AD**, etc., cite the **most specific** rule; use **10.5.5** only when the incident is clearly **sweeping** (or the equivalent in your discipline’s PDF).
3. **Decision:** confirmed **10.5.5** is normally **DQ** during the COF—verify exact outcome in your edition; the competitor does **not** continue after that DQ.
4. **RM / paperwork:** notify **RM**; scoresheet line like **“DQ — Rule 10.5.5 (sweeping)”** + time, witnesses, short description of **muzzle** path vs body.

## IPSC (Jan 2026)

Long-gun books may use different numbering or wording than Handgun; use **10.5.5** in Handgun Jan 2026 as a **review anchor**, then confirm the matching clause in your discipline’s **Jan 2026** PDF.

- Common triggers: reloads tight against the body, “threading” the muzzle past a sleeve/shoulder, long-gun carry without clear **muzzle** control relative to legs.
- If uncertain, **do not guess**—open the PDF and cite the exact sub-rule.

## Common mistakes

- Confusing **sweeping** with a **180° / muzzle safe angle** breach—different anchors.
- Ignoring **10.5.5.1** on handgun: the exception is for **holster** procedures per **division**, not a blanket excuse for muzzle-on-body movement.
- Logging vague language (“almost touched”) without documenting **muzzle** direction relative to the body.
